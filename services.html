Career and Technology Education

Career and Technology Education (CTE) prepares both youth and adults for a wide range of careers. These careers require varying levels of education- from high school and postsecondary certificates, to apprenticeships, or two- and four-year college degrees. Students add value to their overall education by completing CTE programs of study that provide opportunities to earn industry-recognized credentials and college credit while still in high school.

Career and Technical Education is a crucial part of TSA as well as the Fox Chapel Area High School cirriculum. Our school teaches many different courses based on the principles of Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics. STEM are very important subjects in a student's scholastic career, and provides students with the invaluable skills that can be useful in a student's future. Our school offers many STEM courses, and you can view them by clicking on their respective tabs.

Environmental Geomorphology

Description: Environmental Geomorphology is the required science course for all 9th grade students. This course has been divided into the following units of study: the nature of science, meteorology, geology, and hydrology. It is designed to provide students with a physical and chemical understanding of the Earth’s structure and its surrounding environment. When the course is completed, students will have a working knowledge of all aspects of the Earth, its atmosphere, and current environmental issues.

Geomorphology

Description: Geomorphology is the required science course for all 9th grade Molecular Biology students. This course is divided into the following units of study: the nature of science, meteorology, geology, and hydrology. It is designed to provide students with a physical and chemical understanding of the Earth’s structure and its surrounding environment. Upon course completion, students will have a working knowledge of all aspects of the Earth and its atmosphere. In each unit, students are provided with student-driven activities, lectures, demonstrations, and real-life scenarios. Evaluation is based on test scores, collaborative projects, and homework. This version of the course will cover most topics in the 5-day a week course, but at a much faster pace. It is open only to 9th grade students concurrently taking Biology I – Molecular.

Biology I

Description: Biology is the study of LIFE! This is a survey course in which biology is presented from the unity, diversity, and interaction approach. Course topics include: what is life, the nature of science, cellular structure and function, viruses and bacteria, energy, ecology, classification, evolution, the cell cycle, meiosis, classical genetics, and biotechnology.

Biology I - Molecular

Description: This course is a molecular approach to biology and is designed to be an introductory course for college-bound students intending to major in science or a related field. Learning is emphasized by inquiry and application, therefore additional time is provided for laboratory work. The general concepts included are: the scientific method, basic chemistry, biochemistry, cell structure, function and processes, classification, evolution, genetics, and ecological relationships.

Biology II AP

Description: Biology II Advanced Placement is a course for college-bound students who want a second level of biology and is designed to be the equivalent of a two-semester college introductory biology course. This is a survey course covering topics which include molecular-cellular biology, evolution, diversity, basic biology of plants and animals, and ecology. The fundamental biological concepts are studied in depth through a mixture of lectures, discussions, projects, laboratory work and supplemental readings of current scientific literature. Laboratory activities, which correspond to topics being covered in class, have been designed to help students improve their observational skills, learn how to gather and analyze data, and to design experiments.

Ecology

Description: Ecology is the study of interactions between living things and their environments. This course gives students a general overview of human and natural ecology. Topics include: special earth, aquatic and terrestrial biomes, ecosystem form and function, and a brief look at maintaining and sustaining a healthy earth. Class activities include class discussions, demonstrations, online learning, lectures, cooperative group work, small group laboratory activities (indoor and outdoor), and individual work.

Physical Science (Also Offered Online as a Virtual Course)

Description: This course is for juniors and seniors who want additional experience in science but do not have the mathematic prerequisites necessary to take physics. Practical, real-world applications of science are stressed. Topics covered include matter, electricity, magnetism, waves, sound, and light.

Astronomy (Also Offered Online as a Virtual Course)

Description: Astronomy will provide students with theory in nighttime observations of the seasonal skies. In addition to textbook and laboratory related materials, students will work extensively with the high school planetarium. The course will cover the following: earth motions and how those motions affect the day and night sky; constellation location in the northern and southern skies; celestial movements of the sun and moon; planetary location, motion, and comparative planetary geology; and life cycles of stars and galaxies.

Quantitative Chemistry I

Description: This course is designed for college-bound students who are planning to study in a science or science-related field. It is the recommended course for students planning to take Chemistry Il AP.

Chemistry II AP

Description: Chemistry II Advanced Placement is designed for students planning to major in chemistry or related fields of science. The fundamental principles of chemistry are stressed so that students may compete successfully in freshman college chemistry courses. Topics include atomic/molecular structure, states of matter, kinetics, chemical equilibria, acid/base relations, thermochemistry, electrochemistry, and organic chemistry.

Anatomy and Physiology

Description: Anatomy and Physiology is an introductory course for students who are considering a career in an area related to medicine such as nursing, medical technology, or physical therapy. The course will allow the students to make application of human anatomy and physiology to their own lives, as well as giving them a background that will be applicable to their post- secondary education.

Physics I - Mechanics (Also Offered Online as a Virtual Course)

Description: Physics I – Mechanics is an accelerated course designed for college-oriented students who have sound math preparation in accordance with the specified prerequisites. There is a major hands-on component to this class, and the ultimate goal is for students to gain a deep level of understanding of the basic concepts of forces and motion.

Physics II - Electricity and Magnetism (Also Offered Online as a Virtual Course)

Description: This accelerated course is designed for college-oriented students who have successfully completed Physics I. There is a major hands-on component to this class, and the ultimate goal is for students to gain a deep level of understanding of the basic concepts of electromagnetism.

Physics III AP

Description: The topics included in mechanics are vector, motion in one dimension, motion in a plane, particle dynamics, work and energy, the conservation of energy, the conservation of linear momentum, collisions, rotational kinematics, rotational dynamics and the conversation of angular momentum, equilibrium of rigid bodies, oscillations, and gravitation. The topics included in electricity and magnetism are the nature of electric charge and matter, the electric field, Gauss’s law, electric potential, capacitors and dielectrics, current and resistance, electromotive force and circuits, the magnetic field, Ampere’s law, Faraday’s law, inductance, magnetic properties of matter, electromagnetic oscillations, and electromagnetic waves.

Environmental Science AP

Description: This AP course is designed to be equivalent to a one-semester introductory level college course in environmental science as well as human impacts. The course will cover a variety of topics from biology, geology, chemistry, and geography as they relate to environmental science. The course will have a science-based emphasis rather than a social or political emphasis. The topics covered will be similar to Ecology, but will be at a higher level designed to meet the needs of students planning on a career in an environmental-related field or the college-bound student with an interest in environmental science.

Introduction to Technology Systems (Also Offered Online as a Virtual Course)

Description: Students are responsible for completing projects in four systems of technology. These systems include communication, construction, manufacturing, and transportation. Students will participate in technology activities with emphasis placed on planning, design, and problem solving. Individual students or groups of students may undertake projects.

Advanced Technology Systems (Levels 2,3,4)

Description: The Advanced Technology Systems course is derived from the Technology Student Association (TSA) curriculum. Students will have an opportunity to further develop and advance their understanding of technology systems. This course is recommended for college-bound students entering various industrial, technological, or engineering programs, as well as for students who will directly enter the workforce.

Introduction to Production Systems

Description: This course introduces students to the fundamental skills, ideas, methods, and machinery used in the exciting field of industrial design. Through hands-on experiences and problem-based activities, students will discover how each component of the design process impacts another. Activities will center around the following areas of focus: product design and development (prototyp-ing), modes of transportation (including the principles of flight and marine-based design), computer integrated production, and basic construction principles.

Advanced Production Systems (Levels 2,3,4)

Description: Activities will focus on the concepts of product design, mass production, marketing, ergonomics (designing for human use), computer-generated project design, and basic construction techniques. Fundamental skills on machinery and Computer Numerical Control (CNC) equipment are required, and safe usage of these tools will be heavily stressed.

Introduction to Manufacturing Technology

Description: This course is designed to introduce various aspects of manufacturing. Students will experience different manufacturing procedures using handtools, portable power tools, power machines and equipment, various welding equipment, and computers. Emphasis will be placed on processing materials in the areas of metals, plastics, and a variety of industrial materials. Students will be required to work on and produce individual products as well as start and organize a manufacturing company.

Advanced Manufacturing Technology (Levels 2,3,4)

Description: Advanced Manufacturing Technology is a multi-segmented, activity-based curriculum-driven program designed to introduce students to Computer Numerical Control (CNC), robotics, and laser engraving. Student activities are centered around the CAD/CAM software Mastercam, CorelDRAW, and several types of robotics software.

Introduction to Graphics Communications Technology

Description: This course will introduce the students to a variety of ways in which the graphic communications industry produces a final printed product. The three main printing processes the students will work with are digital print production, photo-offset lithography, and photo screen printing. Student projects will include the following processes: digital desktop publishing, layout and design, scanning procedures, process camera, film developing and darkroom procedures, offset plate making, and computer to plate production. Individual student projects will satisfy specific requirements.

Advanced Graphic Communications Technology (Levels 2,3,4)

Description: In this advanced course, the students will have an opportunity to further develop and advance their skills in any phase of the graphic communications industry. They will be given an opportunity to participate in school-related production printing. Through their work duties on various publications and projects, students will have covered a variety of job-oriented skills which can be utilized in the printing industry.

Introduction to Engineering/Drawing/CAD Technology (Also Offered Online as a Virtual Course)

Description: The major units in this course include line techniques, lettering, sketching, orthographic projections; theory of shape and description; one, two and three view drawings, auxiliary, revolved, and section views; and pictorial drawings such as isometric projection, oblique, and one and two point perspective drawings. Also included are surface development, parallel and radial lines, and blueprint production from student-developed tracings. The students’ time will be scheduled to include traditional drafting techniques as well as the development of CAD (Computer Aided Design). This course will be beneficial for all students expressing an interest in the related technical fields of engineering, architecture, drafting/design, construction, or manufacturing. Objectives will be accomplished through lecture and classwork. Student drawings, ability acquired, technique developed, and an understanding of drafting principles (traditional and computer aided) are the basis for grading.

Advanced Engineering/Drawing/CAD Technology (Levels 2,3,4)

Description: Advanced Engineering/Drawing/CAD Technology advances the skills required in architectural drafting. Units include construction details, floor plans, elevations, foundations, wall sections, roofs, design, as well as plot plans, scale model construction, and use of the air brush in renderings. The second part of the semester will include development of the skills required for machine drawings and design. Units include detailed sketching of machine parts, assembly drawings, precision dimensioning, exploded assembly, cams, gears, threads, fasteners, surface development, and intersections. It also covers descriptive geometry and geometric construction as applied to the development of drawings. Students will proceed through both traditional and CAD (Computer Aided Design) techniques in meeting course objectives. Objectives are accomplished through student drawings, a set of working drawings, a scale model, and completion of the units described above. Intergraph microstation software will be utilized in meeting course objectives. This course will also consist of a series of independent study-research activities within the area of a student’s interest, strength, and/or background. Course requirements will include a proposal/contract, a progress report, and a final report. The role of Engineering/Drawing/CAD Technology at this level is to develop creative and problem-solving abilities. Occupational requirements, procedures, practices, and standards will also be covered. Students planning to pursue further study in the technical fields of engineering, drafting, CAD, architecture, design, construction or manufacturing will find this course very useful.

Algebra I

Description: Algebra I is designed for the college- or technical school-bound student needing a basis for work in higher mathematics and to prepare for the SATs. Major topics include tools of algebra, solving equations, solving inequalities, ratio and proportion, probability, percent change, systems of equations and inequalities, properties of exponents, polynomials and factoring, quadratic equations and functions, and radicals.

Geometry Technology Enhanced

Description: This course is designed for students who want to learn the basic concepts of Geometry. Some study in Geometry is recommended for all students taking the SAT college entrance tests. This course involves some emphasis on reasoning and proofs, but not as much as Geometry 3143. Theorems will be introduced through a discovery method. Topics included in the course are areas, Pythagorean Theorem, triangles, quadrilaterals, circles, similarity, parallel and perpendicular lines, and the basic geometric properties including volume, perimeter, and congruence.

Algebra II Technology Enhanced

Description: This course is designed for students who want to learn the basic concepts of Algebra II. It is a functional approach to Algebra II and utilizes an intelligent computer tutor as an integral part of the course. Students are advised that Algebra II contains important material that is included on the SAT. Major units of the course are: linear equations and inequalities, applications of mathematics, equation solving, quadratics, and probability.

Algebra III

Description: Students who would like to improve their algebraic skills and prepare for college mathematics should select this course. This course expands algebraic concepts from Algebra II, including radicals and exponents, and introduces polynomial, rational, exponential, and logarithmic equations and their graphs, as well as systems of equations, sequences, and series.

Pre-Calculus

Description: Pre-Calculus is an advanced level, demanding, fast-paced course. This course is designed to prepare students who plan to pursue college studies in mathematics, engineering, or the sciences. The central theme of Pre-Calculus is functions as models of change and how transformations alter the parent graphs. Pre-Calculus combines both advanced algebra and trigonometric families of functions; each family of functions and their inverse is represented symbolically, numerically, graphically, and verbally. The advanced algebra functions include those that are linear, quadratic, absolute value, square root, cubic, higher power polynomial, rational, exponential, and logarithmic functions. The trigonometric function study includes the six trigonometric functions and their graphs, identities, solving trigonometric equations, radian measure, linear and angular velocity, and problem solving with real-world applications of both right triangles and oblique triangles using the laws of sines and cosines.

Business Calculus

Description: This course offers exposure to the fundamental principles of calculus regarding differentiation and integration. It may serve as an introduction to calculus for those who later plan on taking a first course in scientific calculus, or it may be taken for the purpose of earning college credit through the University of Pittsburgh for those planning on pursuing further studies and/or careers in business, economics, life sciences, and social sciences. The course will cover topics including: limits and their applications, derivatives for the purpose of graphing and optimization, implicit differentiation, business concepts including marginal analysis and elasticity of demand, integration techniques, practical uses of the definite integral, and optimization of multivariable functions.

Calculus AB AP

Description: This is an advanced-level, extremely demanding, fast-paced course for students who are mathematically gifted and highly motivated. This is an intensive study of topics in differential and integral calculus, including limits, differentiation, related rates, implicit differentiation, Newton’s Method, particle motion, differential equations, integration, u-substitution, and solids of revolution. Emphasis is placed on a multirepresentational approach to calculus, with concepts, results, and problems being expressed graphically, numerically, analytically, and verbally. The focus is on the application of these concepts, rather than the manipulation or memorization of an extensive taxonomy of functions, curves, theorems, or problem types.

Calculus BC AP

Description: This is an advanced-level, extremely demanding, fast-paced course for students who are mathematically gifted and highly motivated. It emphasizes more exhaustive problem solving. This is an intensive study of topics in differential and integral calculus, including all topics from Calculus AB Advanced Placement, as well as techniques of integration; improper integrals; sequence and series; and parametric, polar, and vector functions. Emphasis is placed on a multirepresentational approach to calculus, with concepts, results, and problems, being expressed graphically, numerically, analytically, and verbally. The focus is on the application of these concepts, rather than the manipulation or memorization of an extensive taxonomy of functions, curves, theorems, or problem types.

Statistics AP

Description: This course is equivalent to an introductory, non-calculus based, college course in statistics which is typically required for college majors such as engineering, psychology, sociology, health science, and business. The purpose of this course is to introduce students to the major concepts and tools for collecting, analyzing, and drawing conclusions from data. Problem-solving application is emphasized. Topics included in the course are the organization of data, numerical descriptive measures, probability, discrete random variables and their probability distributions, continuous random variables and the normal distribution, sampling distributions, estimation of the mean and proportion, hypothesis tests about the mean and proportion, estimation and hypothesis for two populations, chi-square tests, analysis of variance, and simple linear regression.

Linear Algebra

Description: This course studies the relationships between systems of linear equations, properties of matrices and vectors, properties of vector and inner product spaces, and linear transformations. A topical outline of the course includes systems of linear equations, matrices, determinants, vectors in 2-space and 3-space, Euclidean vector spaces, general vector spaces, inner product spaces, eigenvalues and eigenvectors, linear transformations, and applications.
