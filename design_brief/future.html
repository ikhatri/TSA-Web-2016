Unmanned Aircraft Systems: The future of UAS

Civil Use and Research Purposes

UAS have proven themselves to be useful when used for research. Recently, UAS have been used to collect information that is more accurate than that collected by humans.

A Fire Scouting UAS

After a wildfire burned part of Jackson County, Grand Bay National Estuarine Research Reserve specialists and the U.S. Forestry were able to use drones to photograph the damage, resulting in more accurate information that showed the effect of only the fire on the vegetation rather than a collection of data that would have taken weeks to compile and have had a multitude of other factors that affected the vegetation post fire.

UAS could potentially be used by commercial airlines as replacements for piloted plans. They can also be used to inspect structures such as bridges, dams, and skyscrapers, thereby eliminating the risks involved with having people inspect them. They can be used to advertise and can be used for both the filming of movies and as a means for news to be reported.

The Future of UAS

Currently, UAS are limited in the functions that they can perform autonomously, able to be operated entirely on their own. They are predicted to be able to function with a higher reliance on the system itself rather than on a pilot and to even become fully autonomous when completing pre-programmed tasks.
